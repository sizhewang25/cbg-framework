# VP Selection — Agreement-Methodology Replication — Lessons

## 2026-05-23

Lessons will be captured here as corrections and discoveries occur during the task.

Initial framing (will likely be revisited):

- **"Agreement" without hard GT is a different question than "accuracy" with hard GT.** Cho had no choice — VPN endpoints don't come with verified physical coordinates, so agreement-with-full-pool was the strongest signal available. We do have hard GT, so we should treat "agreement" as a *generalization check* (does the subset preserve the system's behavior?) and treat "accuracy" as the primary quality signal. Reporting only agreement, with our setup, would be throwing away the better evidence.
- **Pool size matters more than relative percentage.** Cho's "32% of pool" headline number is for a 780-anchor pool. With ~12K probes the diversity tail is much fatter and absolute K — not %-of-pool — is the operational quantity. Plan the sweep on log-K, not log-%.
- **`dist_rtt` is structurally limited in our setup.** No probe↔probe RTT mesh = no full RTT graph over the candidate VP pool. Worth running on the 723-anchor subset as a methodological replication of Cho's negative result, but not as our default candidate.
- **Don't lift Cho's 0.51c speed limit verbatim, and don't recalibrate from probes either.** Cho's number was calibrated against their 780-anchor mesh; we recalibrate on our 723-anchor mesh (post-SOI filter) using the existing `LowEnvelopeLTD` per-anchor LP fit. Probes are wrong for this step despite being closer to our actual benchmark data: probe-side last-mile + GT noise survives the SOI filter and would inflate $S$. The per-anchor envelope smooths over single-pair RTT noise. When calibrating a *constant* — as opposed to a per-VP curve — favor the cleanest input, even if it's a smaller pool.

- **Calibrate with the production LP baseline, not a relaxed one.** `RTTDistanceModel.fit()` defaults to `baseline_slope = THEORETICAL_SLOPE = 0.01 ms/km` (= 2/3·c, the production CBG sanity floor). An early instinct was to relax this to `2/c` (vacuum-light) so the calibration could "see" anchors that would otherwise peg at 200 km/ms — but the headline p99 is essentially identical either way (185.56 vs 185.85 km/ms, 0.16% apart), because percentiles are robust to top-tail pile-up. Stick with the production baseline so calibration LP behaviour matches benchmark-run LP behaviour. "Pegged at SOI" is also a cleaner concept than "pegged at vacuum-light after relaxing a production constraint that we'd then re-impose downstream." The pegging-detection still works (6 of 757 anchors flagged in our run) — they're just flagged against the production floor instead of an artificially loosened one.

- **`LowEnvelopeLTD._fit` is the right LP — just don't access it through the wrapper.** The LP itself (`RTTDistanceModel.fit_bestline_lp`) is what we want; the `LowEnvelopeLTD` wrapper doesn't expose enough plumbing (per-anchor records, fitted slopes/intercepts) for analytical use. The script calls `RTTDistanceModel.fit()` directly per anchor, which uses the exact same LP and the exact same default baseline. No methodological asymmetry between calibration and benchmark.

- **SOI sanitization and LP-envelope calibration look at different things.** SOI's per-pair check is `2·d/rtt > 200 km/ms` — it treats *all* of RTT as propagation time. The LP fit is `rtt = slope·d + intercept`, where intercept absorbs per-pair last-mile / setup delay, so `2/slope` is the *marginal* propagation speed at large $d$. Once intercept absorbs ~10 ms of slack, the slope can imply > 200 km/ms (= pegging at the LP floor) even when no individual pair's raw `2d/rtt` exceeds 200 km/ms. Post-SOI data therefore still has anchors where the LP pegs at the SOI floor (6 of 757 in our data). SOI is necessary, not sufficient; LP-floor pegging is the additional check that catches the residual cases.

- **For noisy empirical tails, use p99 instead of max.** With anchor data that's been only structurally filtered (SOI + peg-detection), single anchors can still set the raw max by chance — e.g., one anchor's LP slope of 0.01006 vs another's 0.01000 (= SOI) is one rank apart but doesn't mean anything physical. Cho's "fastest of all" works on clean data; on ours, p99 captures the actual fast-network tail without one bad fit dominating. Our headline S = 185.56 km/ms (p99). +21.3% over Cho's 153 km/ms — plausibly explained by ~3 years of network evolution + different anchor pool.
