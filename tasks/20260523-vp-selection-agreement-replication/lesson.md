# VP Selection — Agreement-Methodology Replication — Lessons

## 2026-05-23

Lessons will be captured here as corrections and discoveries occur during the task.

Initial framing (will likely be revisited):

- **"Agreement" without hard GT is a different question than "accuracy" with hard GT.** Cho had no choice — VPN endpoints don't come with verified physical coordinates, so agreement-with-full-pool was the strongest signal available. We do have hard GT, so we should treat "agreement" as a *generalization check* (does the subset preserve the system's behavior?) and treat "accuracy" as the primary quality signal. Reporting only agreement, with our setup, would be throwing away the better evidence.
- **Pool size matters more than relative percentage.** Cho's "32% of pool" headline number is for a 780-anchor pool. With ~12K probes the diversity tail is much fatter and absolute K — not %-of-pool — is the operational quantity. Plan the sweep on log-K, not log-%.
- **`dist_rtt` is structurally limited in our setup.** No probe↔probe RTT mesh = no full RTT graph over the candidate VP pool. Worth running on the 723-anchor subset as a methodological replication of Cho's negative result, but not as our default candidate.
- **Don't lift Cho's 0.51c speed limit verbatim, and don't recalibrate from probes either.** Cho's number was calibrated against their 780-anchor mesh; we recalibrate on our 723-anchor mesh (post-SOI filter) using the existing `LowEnvelopeLTD` per-anchor LP fit. Probes are wrong for this step despite being closer to our actual benchmark data: probe-side last-mile + GT noise survives the SOI filter and would inflate $S$. The per-anchor envelope smooths over single-pair RTT noise. When calibrating a *constant* — as opposed to a per-VP curve — favor the cleanest input, even if it's a smaller pool.

- **`LowEnvelopeLTD._fit` can't calibrate as-is — its LP baseline is the answer.** `RTTDistanceModel.fit_bestline_lp` defaults to `baseline_slope = THEORETICAL_SLOPE = 0.01 ms/km` (= 2/3·c, the production CBG sanity floor). The LP can't return a slope below this, so calibration via the wrapper class pegs every fast anchor at exactly 200 km/ms — measuring the cap, not the data. The calibration script has to drop down to `RTTDistanceModel` directly and pass `baseline_slope = 2/c = 0.00667 ms/km` so the LP is bounded only by physics. Wrapper convenience hides production assumptions; for calibration, take the LP raw.

- **SOI sanitization and LP-envelope calibration look at different things.** SOI's per-pair check is `2·d/rtt > 200 km/ms` — it treats *all* of RTT as propagation time. The LP fit is `rtt = slope·d + intercept`, where intercept absorbs per-pair last-mile / setup delay, so `2/slope` is the *marginal* propagation speed at large $d$. Once intercept absorbs ~10 ms of slack, the slope can imply > 200 km/ms even when no individual pair's raw `2d/rtt` exceeds 200 km/ms. Post-SOI data therefore still has anchors where the LP pegs at the speed-of-light floor (5 of 752 in our data). SOI is necessary, not sufficient; LP-floor pegging is the additional check that catches the residual cases.

- **For noisy-tail empirical maxes, use p99 instead of max.** With anchor data that's been only structurally filtered (SOI, peg-detection), single anchors still set the raw max by chance (e.g., a Bangalore anchor with n=37 measurements gives 276 km/ms while the next-fastest cluster sits at 186–199 km/ms). Cho's "fastest of all" works on clean data; on ours, p99 captures the actual fast-network tail without being driven by one bad fit. Our headline S = 185.85 km/ms (p99); raw max 276 km/ms is kept as a diagnostic. This is a +21.5% increase over Cho's 153 km/ms (2024 vs ~2022 data; different anchor pool; 3 years of network improvements).
