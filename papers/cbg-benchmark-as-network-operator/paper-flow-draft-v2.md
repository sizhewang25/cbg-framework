# CBG Benchmark — Paper Flow

**Working title:** Constraint-Based Geolocation: Accuracy × Practicality from Network Operator Perspectives

---

## 1. Introduction

Network operators, including ISPs, mobile carriers, and transit providers, continuously serve content from hypergiants and CDNs to their customers. Geolocating the IPs of these hypergiants and CDNs is a routine operational need: traffic monitoring, traffic engineering, and troubleshooting all require knowing where a hypergiant or CDN endpoint physically sits. At operator scale, this means geolocating millions of IPs on a recurring basis as CDN footprints shift and peering arrangements change.

No existing approach fully serves the operator's needs; each falls short in a distinct way. Active measurement-based methods achieve high accuracy but demand probing infrastructure at a cost that is prohibitively expensive at this scale. Commercial geolocation databases are black-box: when a geolocated IP produces a wrong traffic-engineering decision, there is no indication of why and no path to correction. Reverse DNS hostnames and RFC 8805 Geofeed records, self-reported by network providers, suffer from uneven adoption (e..g, some ASNs publish nothing), inconsistent location naming conventions, and records that are stale or deliberately erroneous. Approaches built on publicly available passive datasets do not reflect the operator's actual network topology to its peering partners: public vantage points are not always co-located with operator infrastructure, and mixed-ASN measurements inject noise that does not exist on paths between the operator and a single hypergiant or CDN.

Network operators, however, hold two structural advantages that general-purpose approaches cannot exploit. First, operators know their own network topology and connectivity to peering hypergiants and CDNs, including the private and public peering locations and the partner data centers at each interconnection point. This bounds the geolocation answer space: a CDN IP observed at the operator's core is not at an arbitrary point in continuous latitude and longitude space; it sits at one of a known set of interconnection sites. Therefore, geolocation is recast from coordinate regression to classification over that bounded set. Second, operators collect large volumes of passive RTT measurements to hypergiants and CDNs at in-network vantage points that end users and public measurement platforms do not have visibility into. 

These two advantages make latency-based geolocation the natural fit: it runs entirely on data the operator already collects, requires no active probing, no third-party service, and no cooperation from the target organization. The simplest such approach, Geo-Ping, estimates a target's location as the location of the nearest vantage point, where nearness is measured by latency, but its accuracy is directly bounded by VP density and proximity to the target. Constraint-Based Geolocation (CBG) is a family of variants each of which uses multilateration across all available vantage points, differing in how they model the latency-to-distance relationship and combine constraints, promising higher accuracy by geometrically combining latency constraints from multiple vantage points. Three questions define our benchmark study: *What geolocation accuracy is achievable using Geo-Ping or CBG under operator-realistic deployment conditions? Does CBG provide meaningful gain over Geo-Ping under network operator criteria, and if so, which variant and under what conditions? What does CBG deployment cost in runtime and memory?*

These questions remain under-explored in the existing literature. Existing Geo-Ping/CBG studies are designed for general-purpose IP geolocation, a scope that differs from the operator setting in ways that prevent them from fully answering operator questions. First, they measure error distance against ground truth of globally sampled IP targets, rather than classification accuracy over the bounded location set of data centers of hypergiants and CDNs. Second, they naturally draw on globally distributed vantage points, while most network operators deploy at a national scale only. Third, their target populations are similarly global, while operators primarily geolocate IPs served from local CDN PoPs within their own country. Beyond these scope differences, no cross-variant CBG benchmark exists under operator-realistic conditions, and few studies jointly evaluate accuracy alongside practicality such as runtime and memory consumption. We address these gaps as follows.

We present, to the best of our knowledge, the first cross-variant CBG benchmark from the network-operator perspective, comparing against Geo-Ping as a baseline. Our contributions are:

- **A unified CBG framework for implementation and benchmarking.** We decompose CBG into a unified composable three-phase framework. With it, we reproduce four foundational CBG variants (Vanilla, Speed-of-Internet (SOI), Octant, Spotter) and benchmark them against Geo-Ping under operator-realistic deployment conditions.
- **Priorietary dataset evaluation from a network operator perspective.** We evaluate them under both the coordinate-error metric used in prior work and a classification criterion over the operator's bounded answer space, across two datasets: a proprietary single-operator dataset as the primary evaluation substrate, where per-ASN RTT measurements from distributed network cores provide explanable topology and clean latency data, and a public RIPE Atlas dataset to illustrate the inherent limitations of community datasets for operator-facing geolocation.
- **Failure taxonomy and cost analysis.** We characterize when and why each variant succeeds or fails through a geometric failure taxonomy, quantify the accuracy recovered by the bounded answer space that coordinate metrics miss, and report per-phase runtime and memory costs.
- **Improved CBG variants.** Informed by the analysis, we propose improved CBG variants that outperform the four foundational variants by up to X% in geolocation accuracy.

Together, these contributions provide network operators with actionable guidance on when CBG outperforms Geo-Ping, which variant to select under their deployment conditions, and at what runtime and memory cost, supporting scalable in-house IP geolocation capability for better traffic management.
