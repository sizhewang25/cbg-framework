# CBG Benchmark — Paper Flow

**Working title:** Constraint-Based Geolocation: Accuracy × Practicality from Network Operator Perspectives

---

## 1. Introduction

Network operators, including ISPs, mobile carriers, and transit providers, continuously serve content from hypergiants and CDN providers to their customers. Geolocating the IPs of these hypergiants and CDNs is a routine operational need: traffic monitoring, traffic engineering, and troubleshooting all require knowing where a CDN or hypergiant endpoint physically sits. At operator scale, this means geolocating millions of IPs on a recurring basis as networks evolve.

Existing approaches each fail the operator in a distinct way. Active measurement-based methods achieve high accuracy but demand probing infrastructure at a cost that is prohibitively expensive at this scale. Commercial geolocation databases are black-box: when a geolocated IP produces a wrong traffic-engineering decision, there is no indication of why and no path to correction; they also raise privacy concerns for operators who cannot expose customer-adjacent traffic data to a third party. Reverse DNS hostnames and Geofeed records, self-reported by network providers, suffer from uneven adoption (entire ASNs publish nothing), inconsistent location naming conventions, and records that are stale or deliberately erroneous. Approaches built on publicly available passive datasets do not reflect the operator's actual path to its peering partners: public vantage points are not co-located with operator infrastructure, and mixed-ASN measurements inject noise that does not exist on a single operator-to-hypergiant path.

Network operators, however, hold two structural advantages that general-purpose approaches cannot exploit. First, operators know their own network topology and connectivity to peering hypergiants and CDNs, including the private and public peering locations and the data centers on the other side. This bounds the geolocation answer space: a CDN IP observed at the operator's core is not at an arbitrary point in continuous lat/lon space; it sits at one of a known set of interconnection sites. Geolocation reduces from coordinate regression to classification over that bounded set. Second, operators collect large volumes of passive RTT measurements at in-network vantage points (core routers, user-plane nodes, mobile-core sites) that end users and public measurement platforms do not see.

These two advantages make latency-based geolocation the natural fit: it runs entirely on data the operator already collects, requires no active probing, no third-party service, and no cooperation from the target organization. The simplest such approach, Geo-Ping, estimates a target's location as that of its nearest vantage point by latency, but its accuracy is directly bound by VP density and proximity to the target. Constraint-Based Geolocation (CBG) is a family of variants that each multilaterate across all available vantage points, differing in how they model the latency-to-distance relationship and combine constraints, promising higher accuracy by geometrically combining latency constraints from multiple vantage points. The operational question is whether CBG's added complexity actually pays off over Geo-Ping: *What geolocation accuracy is achievable using Geo-Ping or CBG under operator-realistic deployment conditions? Does CBG provide meaningful gain over Geo-Ping under network operator's criteria, and if so, which variant and under what conditions? What does it cost in runtime and memory?*

These questions remain under-explored in the existing literature. Existing Geo-Ping/CBG evaluations frame the problem as general IP geolocation, measuring coordinate error against arbitrary targets, rather than as classification over a bounded answer space. The VP setups are equally mismatched: academic benchmarks draw on globally distributed vantange points, while most network operators deploy at a national scale only. No cross-variant CBG benchmark exists under operator-realistic conditions, and few jointly evaluates accuracy alongside practicality such as runtime and memory consumption for million-scale IP geolocation.

We present, to the best of our knowledge, the first cross-variant CBG benchmark from the network-operator perspective, comparing against Geo-Ping as a basline. We decompose four fundational CBG variants (Vanilla, Speed-of-Internet (SOI), Octant, Spotter) into a unified composable three-phase framework. We evaluate them under both the coordinate-error metric used in prior work and a classification criterion over the operator's bounded answer space, across two datasets: a proprietary single-operator dataset as the primary evaluation substrate, where per-ASN RTT measurements from distributed network cores provide clean topology and latency data, and a public RIPE-based dataset to illustrate the inherent limitations of community datasets for operator-facing geolocation. We characterize when and why each variant succeeds or fails through a geometric failure taxonomy, quantify the accuracy recovered by the bounded answer space that coordinate metrics miss, and report per-phase runtime and memory costs. Informed by the analysis, we further propose improved CBG variants that outperform the four foundational variants by up to X% in geolocation accuracy. Together, these contributions provide operators with actionable guidance on when CBG justifies its complexity over Geo-Ping, which variant to select under their deployment conditions, and at what runtime and memory cost, supporting the continuous, in-house geolocation of hypergiant and CDN IPs that traffic monitoring, traffic engineering, and troubleshooting at operator scale demand.
